Handmade animation

Luke Jaeger, of Northampton, Mass., makes whimsical animated films that display the breadth of his storytelling and artistic talents. His work includes "5 Dreams," a seven-minute haunting and humorous journey through a nocturnal mind, unfolding in serpentine twists of logic. The film uses a combination of drawing, painting, photography, Xerography, cutout and stop-motion techniques. "5 Dreams" was awarded "Most Technically Innovative Film" at the Ann Arbor Film Festival in 1998.

Jaeger’s animated romp, "Out The Fire," is set to a 1946 calypso tune, and it's about the New York Fire Department. Fire-fighting bulldogs, flaming subways, rocket-powered fire extinguishers and sexy skeletons cavort through this "retro" music video.

We asked him to talk with us about his films and how he makes them.

RETRO-ROCKET: How would you describe your films to someone who has never seen them?

LUKE JAEGER: I like the phrase "handmade animation" because it describes the process as well as the product. The films really are hand-drawn and they look that way.
Sometimes the illusion of movement is achieved by manipulating collaged paper. They all have a structure, and some of them have what you'd call a story. I'm more interested in creating some kind of huge and amazing visual experience than I am in character and plot. It's an approach to animation that I don't see a lot of people practicing in this country. There's a genre that people call "experimental" where it's understood that you're dealing with film as a visual medium, but American animation still tends to be very cartoon-centered. Which is fine, and I love cartoons myself. I see my films as being more in a European vein. People who haven't been exposed to a lot of animation from other parts of the world usually don't know what to make of them.

How did you get started in filmmaking?

Believe it or not, I always knew I wanted to do animation. It took me until college to actually attempt it myself. After college I did some commercial animation and effects work, and it didn't get me much of anywhere career-wise although I met lots of people and learned lots of useful tech stuff. Then I had a work-related injury and spent a couple of weeks on my back and had plenty of time to think about how much nicer it would be to get a job with health insurance. So I took what I'd learned about mattes and mask generation and finagled myself a job in offset printing. This was way back in the stone age when people still used darkrooms and lith film. I kept making films in my evenings and weekends, which was hard. I don't know if it would have been any easier had I stayed in the industry. Probably not, I think.

What is it about film that is unlike other mediums?

When it's projected on a big screen, it's just a beautiful aesthetic experience. It sounds corny, but it's magical. It's increasingly rare to be able to show films to a big crowd in a theater with a great projection and sound system, but when it happens it's unlike anything else. VHS video to me is like convenience food. You want to feed your kids in a hurry, you make frozen fish sticks. You want to show someone your film conveniently, you send them VHS. Everything is so compressed, the color palette is squashed down to nothing, the detail is blown out, the sound is raunchy, people are getting up to answer the phone in the middle of watching your stuff ... it's depressing to me that VHS became the standard format, because it's just so bad. Seeing my films transferred to Beta video is tolerable but watching them on VHS is like eating airplane food. The best thing you can say about it is "this is pretty good for airplane food."

Ann Arbor, by the way, is a festival where they really, really care about film as an aesthetic experience. That's the whole point of what they're doing. There's no star fucking and people acting important and whipping their cel phones around. Everyone is just there because they care what film looks like on a big screen, in a beautiful theater, with a huge crowd. Including the audience, which is not your typical art-film crowd ... is "art-film crowd" one of those contradictions-in-terms, like "jumbo shrimp"? Somehow the Ann Arbor people get folks to turn out in droves to watch these really esoteric films, and the audiences go crazy for them. Some of these films, if you saw them under crummy conditions, would be about as pleasant as a root canal. But they're presented so well that people can really see into them and experience them fully. Presentation matters, and film gives you the opportunity, at least in theory, to really control how your stuff is presented and how things feel when it's shown.

I also like film because it is essentially 19th-century technology. You can figure out how it works just by looking at it. It's based on real and physical things—light and shadows and moving objects. With digital technology, you just have to take it on faith that it will work as advertised, because only about three people in the world can really understand at a fundamental level what is physically going on to make it function.

It's a metaphorical realm—you have an interface that approximates some real-world elements, and under that you have software and OS and hardware that are all metaphors for whatever is happening in the box—even to say that "the image is broken down into a pattern of zeroes and ones" is a metaphor. I happen to be quite comfortable with digital things in general and I do Mac stuff for a living, but I don't find it a conducive environment for art. All those metaphors built into the medium get in the way of creating new ones.

For getting an image onto film, you still can't beat a camera. Even if I enjoyed animating on a computer, to be able to project it in a theater would require digital output to film, which last time I checked cost about $1 per frame. And nobody does digital output to 16mm, so it would cost even more because 35mm is inherently more expensive. Digital theater projection, I suppose, might be viable one day but I just like film. I like how it smells when you put it in the camera.

Who are your biggest influences?

For animation, there's Jan Svankmajer, and Chuck Jones, and Terry Gilliam and the 1930s Fleischer studios. I've also been influenced by a lot of comic book artists, like the early MAD guys Wally Wood and Bill Elder, I love the way they just cram every picture full of detail. I can come back to those and read them over and over and roar with laughter every time.
I've always loved Jack Kirby, the way his drawings are right inside that iconic classic 1960's look but also expanding the confines of that look. And Kirby's drawings really move—the figures are always posed in these extreme postures, very theatrical, expressionist hand gestures. I also love Ben Katchor, who does these very dream-like, happy/sad comics about old Jewish guys in vanished New York. Those are so much like the comics I wish I could do that it's a relief to see someone else doing it better than I could.

Animation is very tied in to music for me, and a lot of my influences come from musicians. James Brown, Fela Kuti, Al Green, 1930s jazz.

Tell us a little about the way you work. Do you work alone or with a team?

Mostly alone, pressing friends, relatives, acquaintances and strangers into service when necessary. Stop-motion is good to have help with because it's a kind of performance. Drawn animation, if you're better organized than me, you can give someone a scene and tell him what to do with it. Sometimes I've been able to pull this off.

Where has your work been shown?

Lots of festivals, a few classrooms and theatrical engagements. “Out the Fire” was shown in a firehouse at the Woods Hole festival, which I thought was wonderful. I've had lots of Internet distributors contact me, but I've yet to have someone explain to me where the money is supposed to come from.

What are some of the biggest challenges facing independent filmmakers?

Too much commercialism. The independent film thing has been a bandwagon for so many people. So often, when I tell people that I'm not expecting to get rich doing this, they look at me with a blank expression. It's an expensive medium to work in, so it would be nice if I had bigger budgets—I'd love to work in 35mm some day.

Are there any future screenings of your work that we can look forward to?

"Old Joe's Hittin' the Jug," the second in a series of "antique music videos"—"Out the Fire" being the first—is at the lab right now, and will premiere November 3 at the Northampton Film Festival. This film combines animation, puppets, stop motion and live action and was shot outdoors in black and white at various industrial sites in Northampton.

Do you have any projects in the works? Could you tell us about them?

My life is a crazy roller coaster ride right now—at 36, with three kids, I've chucked my day job and gone back to grad school to get my MFA (at Mass Art in Boston), and so my story of "what I'm doing right now" changes weekly.

I'm actually leaning toward something involving kinetic sculpture or installation art. Which might wind up incorporating film in some way. Or who knows. As the Magic Eight Ball says, "Ask again later."
