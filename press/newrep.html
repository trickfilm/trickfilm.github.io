PRESS/NEW REPUBLIC


What Dreams May Come?
by Jesse Lichtenstein
Post date 10.17.00 "5 Dreams"
Mixed media animation
Director: Luke Jaeger
Country: USA
Running Time: 7:08

It's October, which means it's film festival time here in New York City. And, it turns out, in Austin, Los Angeles, Atlantic City, Fort Lauderdale, the Hamptons, Denver, Seattle.... The Silverlake neighborhood of Los Angeles just had one of its own. The RESFEST 2000 digital film tour has already hit San Francisco, Seattle, London, and Chicago, and will soon head back to New York for points north and far east. America is beset by an epidemic of film festivals--one that appears to be spreading well beyond what used to be film's acknowledged quarantines.

The explosion of film production has been declared a renaissance, a resurgence, a democratic revolution in movies. The agent provocateur, we're told, is the arrival of cheap technology--capable of producing high-quality digital images--in the hands of legions of aspiring filmmakers. Nor are the benefits of digital technology limited to film's means of production. Thanks to the Internet, an unprecedented amount of film is available to an audience of unprecedented scale. Cyberspace has become one giant film festival: the world's largest, which never packs up, never stops screening, and turns away relatively little of what's submitted for viewing.
It is a festival with certain shortcomings, of course. If, as the purists believe, watching movies on video cripples the art form, then watching them on a three-square-inch swath of computer screen would seem to deliver an effective coup de grace. (How would Tarkovsky's Andrei Rublev fare on streaming video?) But let's not get hung up on the current hitches in the technology. Let's concede that in terms of cinematography--of the force and power of the moving image--streaming online film remains crude. The important leap of online film at this point is one of access, of allowing countless sketches to exist where there was formerly blank space.

Luke Jaeger's witty, surreal, and inventive "5 Dreams" is one such creation. Like many of the offerings at Sputnik7, it is a short film: a thoroughly enjoyable way to spend 7 minutes and 8 seconds of your life (or even 21 minutes and 24 seconds--I got a good deal more out of the third viewing than the first). And like many of the short films around the Net, it is animated. It's just the sort of film that might catch your eye at a film festival then disappear into oblivion. Digital technology gives it new and expansive life in cyberspace.

Jaeger has both excellent absurdist instincts and an ability to subtly reproduce the impeccable internal logic of dreams. A narrator (David R. Lenson)--alternately deadpan, sarcastic, and silly--leads us through the dream sequence. On first viewing, the scenes appear to be a series of non sequiturs: the narrator attends a wedding at a seedy hotel, Arnold Schwarzenegger is mistaken for a blind man and abducted from a trendy café, a terrorist complains about the illogic of irrigation pipe arrangement, a subway tunnel suspended beneath a boat threatens to sink, and more. But the voiceover continues seamlessly from one episode to the next, and the animation makes subtle transitions independent of the spoken narrative. Figures or ideas from one dream emerge altered in another, much the way dreams alter waking experience with their own internal logic and language: the Japanese subway station echoes the appearance of a New York subway tunnel in a previous dream; irrigation pipes evoke imagined subterranean burrows full of mail--all very strange, but linked by association among and throughout the dream narratives. And then there are certain non sequiturs which are left unexplained: the surrealism of dream-world weirdness that has no waking analog. The result is a blurring of dream boundaries and a collapse of sensible narrative; "5 Dreams"--whichever five of the flurry Jaeger presents that you choose to count--inhabits a space between sequential episodes and montage.

Visually, Jaeger's mixed-media animation is consistently interesting. Crayon seems to be the dominant agent, but Jaeger employs a range of stop-motion animation, xerography, photography, and cutout techniques to great effect. One of the most impressive moments of this short is a simulation of fancy camera work that displays a mastery of shifting perspective by someone with an obvious awareness of these details. Were this shot on film, the brief scene in the Tokyo subway station--with its pans, dips, and weaves past and through colonnades, its ascents and descents--would be breathtaking and probably physically impossible, at least barring the invention of the indoor helicopter. The other way to make a shot like this one work--using computer-generated imaging--would certainly be beyond the budget of a short film. As "5 Dreams" shows, someone with little money but a mean crayon-hand and a lot of time can achieve a splendid version of images that typically require expensive equipment and large crews.

Not everything in this film matches the originality of its high points. There are dead spots in the script: for instance, the narrator's question, "Haven't you always wondered where letters go after you mail them?" treads perilously close to a bad stand-up comedy routine. His delivery, however, is generally dead-on elsewhere. Add the excellent soundtrack (featuring "If Wynton Marsalis Had a Toy Boat, Would It Sink?") and adept animation, and there's always something worth attending to. Watch this movie, too, for the brief, haunting scene in the bathroom in which the dreamer discovers that the tiles in his bathtub are squares from the AIDS quilt. In the space of a few seconds, the scene achieves an unexpected elegance in such a rough-hewn and consciously eclectic animation-style.

There is no one film that can hope to encapsulate the breadth of online film, which is enormous and growing. But "5 Dreams" is in some sense representative of many films online today: a creation that links old production technique to new distribution technology with idiosyncrasy and surprise. Jaeger's film gives us a sense of the kinds of quirky dreamworlds now available for our consideration twenty-four hours a day. It remains to be seen whether the Net will simply present the same fare as film festivals or whether the new container will begin to shape the contents. This may not make films of the digital era any more exotic than Luke Jaeger's crayons, but at least serious film watchers will be able to witness the skill with which he wields them. The revolution may not be televised--but it will be streamed.

JESSE LICHTENSTEIN writes and works in film. He splits time between Oregon, Boston, and New York.
