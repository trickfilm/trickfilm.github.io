In stop time, Jaeger’s world revs with personality
by LARRY PARNASS

The building blocks of Luke Jaeger’s animations rush by at 24 images a second. They hurry past to make us believe a lot of things that might never enter our minds, were it not for film’s power to hijack our vision. We come to believe, in Jaeger’s “Big Concrete Place,” that a man with a screw loose, his soul burdened by debt, can be blown with his unpaid bills out a window and be the better for it. In his “5 Dreams,” we watch a subway train rattle along in its tunnel under a river, suspended from a toy boat above.

We see in such short films the rush of Jaeger’s haunted and absurdist imagination, not the repose of its many pieces. We get the dance, not the stance.

For someone unaccustomed to gallery shows, the Northampton filmmaker has had a lot of work in frames. His current exhibit at the Northampton Center for the Arts slows his fecund world down to one frame at a time and, in surprising ways, entertains both with its simple ingredients and its peculiar whimsy.
The show comes as Jaeger, 36, prepares to unveil a new animated film, “Old Joe’s Hittin’ the Jug,” at next month’s Northampton Film Festival and as he moves to make art more central to his life. After years of holding jobs just to pay bills, he’s now enrolled in a master of fine arts program at the Massachusetts College of Art in Boston. He hopes to parley the credential into a position teaching studio art. “I realized a few years ago that nobody’s making me do this,” he said of his art, over coffee the other day at the Haymarket Cafe in Northampton. “It’s like coming in from the cold. This isn’t what I do on evenings and weekends any more. It’s really what I do. It’s like learning to say the words and look at yourself in a certain way, and take it seriously.” For Jaeger, the Center for the Arts show, curated by Pam Glaven, is a kind of coming-out party for the artist’s humorous and idea-driven works _ a kind of pop art with a post-industrial hangover. The pieces arrive not with fanfare but as an earnest cross-section of a decade in his creative life. He and Glaven have included samples of the things that crowd an animator’s attic _ ephemera, film production stills, props and masks. Because it stands 15-feet tall, they had to place one piece, the marionette Bob that stars in “Old Joe,” in the performance space outside the gallery door.
A videocassette recorder and TV is set up to play three of Jaeger’s films _ the two mentioned and “Out the Fire,” based on a calypso recording he first heard on a 78-rpm disk owned by his grandparents. In one corner, he invites visitors to try the hand-cranked Victrola on which that song played. Why this recording? It made an impression on him as a child. As an adult, he says he asked, “Why can’t these guys get a music video? What about all that recorded music that’s been forgotten?”

Jaeger says he sees ethnic music as the “secret sub-basement of pop music.” The allure of Jaeger’s film is that he takes us rummaging around down there with him.

He got the idea for his newest film after hearing a jazz tune from the 1930s while listening one day to WMUA in Amherst. “I said, ‘This is cartoon music and I have to find it.’”

Films central

While it’s easy to walk past the VCR, the films are a vital part of the show. In 10 minutes, viewers not only get a way to look at Jaeger’s prints and drawings, they get a reason. He is at heart a performance artist. The logistics are on the walls here. The laughs are on tape. Because the films took so long to create, they seem to stand like little epochs in Jaeger’s creative life. Many of the framed works, including of course the production stills from the films, seem to fall into those categories.

In the hanging works, we see images of the landscapes that seemed to spur Jaeger’s imagination as a young man, from subterranean passages in his native New York City to highway overpasses twisted and ruined by an earthquake in the Bay Area, where he lived before coming with his wife, Shoshana Marchand, to Northampton eight years ago. In a note to gallery visitors, Jaeger says he was mesmerized, as a child, by the crumbling cityscapes around him in New York, as that city struggled with financial ruin. He calls it the landscape of the “formerly useful.” That bitter notion finds its way into other works here, including his drawing, “The last gainfully employed lithographer in America.” In the best of Jaeger’s drawings, we’re won not by his draftsmanship, which can appear hurried, but by the sly humor of his vision. That last lithographer sits at desk with a pair of scissors, carrots, a slice of pizza and other objects and appears to wear a tray on his head that holds three leafy plants.

In some pieces, Jaeger seems to have rushed on to his next idea. The pieces show a comfort and confidence, though, and a strong sense of balance in composition.

When places speak

Jaeger first tried to capture what he saw around him through cartooning. He enrolled at Yale University with plans to study animation as an art major, then opted to shift to an art school. He worked in commercial animation for a time in New York and San Francisco, but ended up taking a string of jobs divorced from the projects that were capturing his imagination nights and weekends.

“It’s part of my long and not illustrious history of day jobs,” he said. He was working at a San Francisco print shop when he began the film “Big Concrete Place.” An old concrete plant nearby had been speaking to him, in that manner of places that can’t stand still. Jaeger invented the notion of a robot-like man who inhabits such a “formerly useful” place _ a man that’s a misfit. The film propels the character through a series of emotional ups and downs.

In some ways, it seems a maiden voyage for an animator, but shows a technical proficiency. Like all animated films, even a short work is a big undertaking. As with all his works, he holds “coloring parties” at which friends help rough out the frames that will be shot in sequence to make figures move. It was edited the old-fashioned way, with cuts and splices. Sound was added later.

After years of thinking in pictures, he says he was confronted with the difficulty of building sound into his story. “I saw this learning curve yawning before me of sound effects design,” he said. “If you want a real esoteric sound, you’ve got to close your eyes and meditate and decide what’s going to make that sound.”

Dreams in motion

After moving to Northampton, Jaeger and Marchand collaborated on a project that seems to have been stimulated by the nightmarish themes in the filmmaker’s work. They began publishing a ‘zine called “Dream World News,” in which they gave accounts of people’s actual dreams, written in the no-nonsense style of the wire services. “People believe what they read when it’s presented as news,” Jaeger said. Over the years, they published six issues.

He decided to play with that material in his “5 Dreams,” a fast, funny and image-rich film with a narration that runs through a fistful of bizarre plots. With a narration by University of Massachusetts literature professor David Lenson, the film plays like a demented documentary. One brief stop-motion sequence in the film, of that subway train running through a tunnel, took Jaeger and two friends seven hours to shoot. The bit had come from a friend’s actual dream.

The gallery show includes the lyrics to a song that dream produced, entitled, “If Wynton Marsalis Had a Toy Boat, Would It Sink?” For good measure, if you snoop around enough, you can find the subway cars that ran through that underground tunnel and a print of the toy boat itself, with its main housing no more than a black cassette tape. The sequence that starts the film demands repeat viewing, if the flood of images is to gain any purchase in our minds. “Stuff goes ripping by. You can’t absorb it in one sitting,” he said of that series.

For Jaeger, the Center for the Arts show offers a way to freeze the hectic pace of his movies and invite people to take a sustained look. He’s opened the door on his quirky cabinet of wonder.
